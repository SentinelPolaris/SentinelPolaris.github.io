This guide is intended for new ECE students who want to purchase a new laptop due to the need of more computing power on the go. I would weight the hardware performance of the laptop more than other factors in the guide and I hope this guide would be able to help you picking you next laptop.
This guide is written under the assumption that you would not have a personal desktop at your disposal to back up your need so every laptop I recommend would have a discrete Nvidia graphic card for better rendering capability in software such as SolidWorks, and additional feature, such as CUDA, which would be used latter in some of your latter courses, such ECE180 GPU programming.

Standards used to pick laptops

Here are some geneal rules I used in determining which laptop I would recommend.

8 gigabytes and more RAM: 64 bit system is required for most of the work you do. MatLab refuses to run on system with a 32 bit system(4G ram and lower); SolidWorks tend to give you low memory warning frequently when your system has less than 8G of ram. Instructor K. W. Morris’s laptop has 8G of ram and you would still be able to see low memory warning when he gives demonstrations.

Nvidia graphics card: Nvidia GPU tend to be more powerful and power efficient than its competitors. Also CUDA binary does not run on AMD graphics cards.

8th gen and above Intel CPU: most if not all of the 8th gen and above Intel CPUs have four physical cores comparing the two physical cores of all previous generation while maintaining similar power consumption and pricing(thanks to the pressure from the AMD side).

Solid State Drive: Faster disk accessing speed is always great.

15 inch screen: 15 inch screen laptop is nicely balanced between weight, price and performance. Also larger than 13 inch screen is beneficial for writing codes.

Why MacBooks are not in the list: Generally speaking Mac books are less powerful hardware wise comparing to laptops from other manufacturers(at same price point), and there are several major issues of Macs that renders them less effective.

  • A lot of software you will be using as engineers are Windows only
  • Macs do not have discrete GPU on most models
  • Apple's philosophy on minimalist I/O is not beneficial for engineers
  • When under pressure, Macs have the tendency to lower CPU speed to cope with thermal issues rather than ramping up fans to dissipate the heat. Essentially, Macs would not be able to maintain advertised clock speed for an extended duration of time. Gentries in Starbucks don't mind trading performance for lower noise and aesthetics but you do.
